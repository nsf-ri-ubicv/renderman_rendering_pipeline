1) Mixed up categories.
This is a first nugget, about the result of mixing up categories. Shown are test accuracy performance statistics for a bunch of two-level (gridded gabor/random) models. There are three problems (the tenway problem, cars vs planes, and furniture (chairs and tables) vs. animals (dogs and cats and reptiles) vs. vehicles (cars and boats and planes).

The control task is the actual category, while the "mixed up" task is one where the 3dmodels in the category have been shuffled, e.g. cars vs planes becomes [one group of mixed cars and planes] vs [another random of cars and planes].

Results are further broken down by which invariances were used: All, meaning translation, scaling, and inplane and out-of-plane rotation; translation only; and inplane rotation only.
The key thing to notice is that rotation is much harder than translation; and that mixing up the categories has an effect. (I will write more text later.)
2) Animals, Guns & Faces.
Below is an exploration of the 45 two-way category (2-CAT) comparisons contained in the 10-way class comparison above. I evaluated 5 models for each two comparison, with the control (non-mixed-up) task with both translation-only and in-plane-rotation only, and the mixed-up task with translation only. I've plotted the mean values for each, and differences between the first and second (T-I) and first and third (T-M). I'm especially interested in identifying 2-CAT problems for which T, T-I and T-M are all high, because these should be the simplest "carriers of the phenotype" of what seems to be biggest problem facing the Thor models.

So let's notice a couple of things.

  1. Some two-problems are much harder than others for rotational invariance.

  2. Animals seem to be screwing everything up. Sort on "T-I" in the table to the right.

  3. Face and guns make things easier. Sort on "Trans".

This suggests that probably study e.g. reptiles vs. planes or cats_and_dogs is a reasonable simplification.
